PhD student of neuroscience at [NYU](https://shanghai.nyu.edu/academics/graduate/neural-science-phd-program) (Shanghai track, advised by [Sukbin Lim](https://shanghai.nyu.edu/academics/faculty/directory/sukbin-lim)). I did my master's at [UChicago](https://masters.cs.uchicago.edu/) computer science, advised by [Yuxin Chen](https://yuxinchen.org). 

I started out working on the machine learning theory topic of "machine teaching" where we studied how humans or machines could efficiently train and influence a machine or human learner. Then I came to the realization that both the learner and teacher can be more broadly defined. The brain can undoubtedly be seen as a learner, but could it be its own teacher at the same time? Indeed, top-down modulations such as attention do modify sensory inputs upstream, such that for the downstream brain areas, "training" data has been optimized by the brain itself to some extent. My current research questions are therefore to understand the validity of this self-teaching heuristic, and to find out ways in which the brain, or a theoretical learning agent, can accomplish self-teaching.
