Fisher information (FI) is a commonly used measure of information in neuroscience. It is so fundamental that many papers just directly apply related results without fully explaining the underlying framework, and yet the concept of FI is also intricate enough to cause confusion for those who have only encountered it for a few times. For example, when I read that FI takes the form of $f'(\theta)^2$, it wasn't clear to me why that is, or what $f(\theta)$ is, even with the footnote explaining that it is the tuning function.  

Here I will explain what it means when someone says that $J(\theta) = f'(\theta)$ if noise is Gaussian or that $J(\theta) = f'(\theta)^2 / f(\theta)$ when Poisson spiking is assumed. On the way, I will also attempt to explain in simple words and illustrations how FI is related to concepts like neural tuning, representational geometry, perceptual discrimination, etc. The framework I use is based on Kriegeskorte and Wei's 2021 review paper, but the hope is that you do not need it to understand this post.


Fisher Information (FI) is defined as the expected precision of (an estimator of) stimulus $\theta$, and is formally expressed as:
\begin{align}
  J(\theta) = \int{-\frac{d^2}{d\theta}\log p(r|\theta)p(r)dr}
\end{align}
If we assume stimulus-independent Gaussian noise with variance 1, then FI is simply:
\begin{align}
  J(\theta) = f'(\theta)^2
\end{align}
, where f(\theta) is the tuning function.
If we assume Poisson spiking, then FI can be shown to have the following form instead:
\begin{align}
  J(\theta) = \frac{f'(\theta)^2}{f(\theta)}
\end{align}

Proof for (2):
When $p(r|\theta)$ is Gaussian:
\begin{align}
  \log p(r|\theta) = -\frac{(r-f(\theta))^2}{2\sigma^2} + const\
  J(\theta) &= -E_{r|\theta}[\frac{d^2}{d\theta^2}\log p(r|\theta)]\
            &= -E_{r|\theta}[\frac{d^2}{d\theta^2}(\frac{rf(\theta)}{\sigma^2}-\frac{f(\theta)^2}{2\sigma^2})]\
            &= -E_{r|\theta}[\frac{rf''(\theta)}{\sigma^2}-(\frac{2f(\theta)f'(\theta)}{2\sigma^2})']\
            &= -E_{r|\theta}[\frac{rf''(\theta)}{\sigma^2}-\frac{f'(\theta)^2}{\sigma^2}-\frac{f(\theta)f''(\theta)}{\sigma^2}]\
            &= -E[-\frac{1}{\sigma^2}f'(\theta)^2]\
  J(\theta) &= f'(\theta)^2
\end{align}

(7) to (8) is because $E_{r|\theta}(r) = f(\theta)$. 
(8) to (9) is because we assume $\sigma = 1$.

Proof for (3) follows the same procedure. If $p(r|\theta)$ is Poisson:
\begin{align}
  \log p(r|\theta) = \log \frac{f(\theta)^re^{-f(\theta)}}{r!} = r\log f(\theta) - f(\theta) - \log r!\
  J(\theta) &= -E_{r|\theta}[r(\frac{f'(\theta)}{f(\theta)})'-f''(\theta)]\
            &= -E_{r|\theta}[r \frac{f''(\theta)f(\theta)-f'(\theta)^2}{f(\theta)^2}-f''(\theta)]\
            &= -[f''(\theta)-\frac{f'(\theta)^2}{f(\theta)}-f'(\theta)]\
  J(\theta) &= \frac{f'(\theta)^2}{f(\theta)}
\end{align}
