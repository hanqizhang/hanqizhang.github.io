Fisher information (FI) is a commonly used measure of information in neuroscience. It is so fundamental that many papers just directly apply related results without fully explaining the underlying framework, and yet it is also intricate enough for those who have only encountered it for a few times. Indeed you could go to Brunel and Nadal's work and read their old notations in much detail, but it could be an overkill for you. Here I will attempt to explain how FI is related to concepts like neural tuning, representational geometry, perceptual discrimination, etc., with an emphasis on filling in oft-omitted derivations (you will see that they are indeed simple, but not obvious) and explicitly highlighting connections among concepts in FI's neighborhood. The framework I use is based on Kriegeskorte and Wei's 2021 review paper, but hopefully you do not have to read it to understand this post.

Fisher Information (FI) is defined as the expected precision of (an estimator of) stimulus $\theta$, and is formally expressed as:
\begin{align}
  J(\theta) = \int{-\frac{d^2}{d\theta}\log p(r|\theta)p(r)dr}
\end{align}
If we assume stimulus-independent Gaussian noise, then FI is simply:
\begin{align}
  J(\theta) = f'(\theta)^2
\end{align}
, where f(\theta) is the tuning function.
If we assume Poisson spiking, then FI can be shown to have the following form instead:
\begin{align}
  J(\theta) = \frac{f'(\theta)^2}{f(\theta)}
\end{align}

Proof for (2):
When p(r|\theta) is Gaussian,
\begin{align}
  \log p(r|theta) = -\frac{(r-f(\theta))^2}{2\sigma^2} + const \
  J(\theta) &= -\mathbb{E}_{r|\theta}[\frac{d^2}{d\theta^2}\log p(r|\theta)] \
  J(\theta) &= f'(\theta)^2
\end{align}
